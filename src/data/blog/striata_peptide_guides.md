## The Complete Beginner's Guide to Peptides: What They Are, How They Work and Why Everyone's Talking About Them

You have probably heard the word "peptide" thrown around in fitness circles, anti-aging conversations or across social media. But what actually are they? And why are so many people, from elite athletes to everyday professionals, adding them to their health protocols?

If you have been curious but not sure where to start, this guide is for you. No jargon. No gatekeeping. Just a clear, honest breakdown of what peptides are and why they matter.

---

### What Is a Peptide?

A peptide is a short chain of amino acids. Amino acids are the building blocks of protein, and when two or more of them link together, they form a peptide. When a chain grows long enough, it becomes what we call a protein.

Think of it like this: amino acids are individual Lego bricks, peptides are small structures built from a handful of those bricks, and proteins are the large, complex constructions made from thousands of them.

Your body already produces hundreds of peptides naturally. Hormones like insulin and oxytocin are peptides. Growth hormone-releasing hormone is a peptide. Even the signals your cells use to communicate with each other are often peptide-based.

So when we talk about using peptides for health and performance, we are not introducing something foreign to the body. We are working with compounds that are structurally very similar to what your body already knows how to use.

---

### How Do Peptides Work?

Peptides work by binding to specific receptors on the surface of cells and triggering a biological response. This is sometimes described as a "lock and key" mechanism: the peptide is the key, and the receptor is the lock.

Depending on which receptors they target, peptides can:

- Signal the pituitary gland to release growth hormone
- Promote the repair of damaged tissue and connective structures
- Regulate appetite and metabolism
- Support immune function
- Influence mood, cognition and sleep

Because peptides are so targeted in how they work, they tend to produce specific, predictable effects rather than broad, system-wide changes. This is one of the reasons they have attracted so much attention in research and performance contexts.

---

### Why Are Peptides Different from Steroids or Hormones?

This is one of the most common questions, and it is a good one.

Anabolic steroids work by flooding the body with synthetic hormones, often overriding natural production and disrupting hormonal balance. The side effect profile can be significant, and the suppression of natural hormone production is a real concern.

Most peptides work differently. Rather than replacing hormones, many peptides stimulate your body to produce or release its own hormones. A growth hormone secretagogue, for example, does not deliver growth hormone directly. It signals your pituitary gland to produce and release more of it naturally. This is a fundamentally different mechanism, and it is why peptides are generally considered to have a more favourable side effect profile than exogenous hormone use.

That said, peptides are not without considerations. Dosing, sourcing and protocol design all matter. Which is why education is important.

---

### What Are the Main Categories of Peptides?

The peptide world is broad, but it broadly organises into a few key categories:

**Growth Hormone Peptides**
These include compounds like CJC-1295, Ipamorelin, GHRP-6 and Tesamorelin. They work by stimulating the release of growth hormone, which supports fat loss, muscle recovery, sleep quality and cellular repair.

**Healing and Recovery Peptides**
BPC-157 and TB-500 fall into this category. They are widely researched for their role in accelerating tissue repair, reducing inflammation and supporting injury recovery.

**Weight Loss and Metabolic Peptides**
GLP-1 receptor agonists like Semaglutide and Tirzepatide have transformed the conversation around obesity medicine. AOD-9604 and HGH Fragment 176-191 are also used in this space.

**Anti-Aging and Longevity Peptides**
GHK-Cu, Epithalon and FOX04-DRI are among the most researched here. They target mechanisms like telomere length, cellular senescence and collagen production.

**Nootropic and Mood Peptides**
Semax, Selank, DSIP and PE-22-28 are used to support cognitive function, reduce anxiety, improve sleep and stabilise mood.

**Hormonal Health Peptides**
PT-141, Kisspeptin, Gonadorelin and Oxytocin are used to support sexual health, testosterone regulation and hormonal balance.

### How Are Peptides Administered?

Most research peptides are administered via subcutaneous injection, which means injecting just under the skin (similar to how diabetics use insulin). The injections use very fine, short needles and are generally well tolerated.

Some peptides are available in nasal spray form, such as Semax and Selank. Others are being developed as oral formulations, though the digestive system tends to break down peptides before they can be absorbed, which is why injection remains the most reliable delivery method for most compounds.

---

This is a nuanced area, and the answer depends on where you are, what the peptide is and how it is being used.

In South Africa, most peptides exist in a grey regulatory area. They are sold as research compounds rather than pharmaceutical products, which means they are not approved drugs but are also not explicitly scheduled substances in most cases.

Some peptides, such as Semaglutide, are regulated pharmaceutical products when used as prescription medications. Others, like BPC-157, have no pharmaceutical approval anywhere in the world but are widely used in research contexts.

### What Makes a Peptide High Quality?

Not all peptides are created equal. Quality varies significantly depending on the manufacturer, and poor quality peptides can be ineffective at best and harmful at worst.

When evaluating a peptide supplier, the key things to look for are:

- **Third-party testing**: Is there a Certificate of Analysis (COA) from an independent laboratory?
- **Purity**: Is the peptide at least 98% pure?
- **Manufacturing standards**: Was it produced in a facility that follows Good Manufacturing Practice (GMP) guidelines?
- **Transparency**: Does the supplier openly share testing data?

We will cover how to read a Certificate of Analysis in detail in a separate article. For now, the short version is: always ask for proof of purity before purchasing from any supplier.

## Peptide Storage 101: How to Reconstitute, Store and Handle Your Peptides the Right Way

You have sourced high-quality peptides. You have done your research on the protocol. But now you are staring at a small vial of white powder and wondering what to do next.

This is where many beginners get stuck. Peptide handling is not complicated, but it does require care. Done correctly, it protects the potency of the compound and ensures accurate dosing. Done carelessly, it can degrade the peptide before you ever use it.

This guide walks you through everything you need to know about reconstituting, storing and handling peptides safely.

---

### Why Do Peptides Come as a Powder?

Most research peptides are supplied in lyophilised form, which is essentially freeze-dried. This is done deliberately. In dry powder form, peptides are far more stable and have a much longer shelf life than in liquid form.

Once a peptide is reconstituted (mixed with a liquid), it becomes more vulnerable to degradation. Temperature, light and contamination all become factors you need to manage.

---

### What You Will Need Before You Start

Before reconstituting any peptide, make sure you have the following:

**Bacteriostatic water (BAC water)**
This is sterile water with 0.9% benzyl alcohol added. The benzyl alcohol acts as a preservative and prevents bacterial growth in the vial after it has been opened. Do not use plain sterile water or tap water. BAC water is available from most medical suppliers and online.

**Syringes and needles**
You will need insulin syringes (typically 1ml capacity) for drawing up and injecting the reconstituted peptide. These are fine-gauge, short needles and are widely available.

**Alcohol swabs**
To sterilise vial tops and injection sites before use.

**A clean, flat surface**
Good hygiene practices matter throughout this process.

---

### How to Reconstitute a Peptide: Step by Step

**Step 1: Let the vial reach room temperature**
If your peptide has been stored in the freezer or fridge, allow it to come to room temperature before opening or handling. This reduces condensation inside the vial.

**Step 2: Swab the vial tops**
Use an alcohol swab to clean the rubber stoppers on both your peptide vial and your BAC water vial. Allow them to air dry for a few seconds.

**Step 3: Draw up your BAC water**
Using a clean syringe, draw up the amount of BAC water you plan to add. The volume of water you use will determine the concentration of your reconstituted peptide. (See the calculation guide below.)

**Step 4: Inject slowly down the side of the vial**
Gently insert the needle through the rubber stopper of the peptide vial. Angle the needle so the BAC water runs down the inside wall of the vial rather than spraying directly onto the powder. This is important. Aggressive mixing can damage the peptide structure.

**Step 5: Do not shake**
Once the water is in, swirl the vial gently until the powder is fully dissolved. Never shake it vigorously.

**Step 6: Inspect the solution**
The reconstituted peptide should be a clear liquid with no visible particles. If it appears cloudy or has floating debris, do not use it.

---

### Working Out Concentration and Dosing

This is where many beginners get confused, but it is straightforward once you understand the relationship between the amount of BAC water you add and the resulting concentration.

**Example:**
You have a 5mg vial of BPC-157. You add 2ml of BAC water.

Your concentration is: 5mg / 2ml = 2.5mg per ml, or 2500mcg per ml.

If your target dose is 250mcg per injection, you would draw up 0.1ml (10 units on an insulin syringe).

Write this calculation down and keep it with your vial. It is easy to forget, especially when you are managing multiple compounds.

---

### How to Store Peptides Before and After Reconstitution

**Lyophilised (unreconstituted) peptides:**
Store in the freezer for long-term storage (up to 12 to 24 months depending on the compound). Store in the fridge if you plan to use within a few weeks. Keep away from light and moisture.

**Reconstituted peptides:**
Store in the refrigerator at 2 to 8 degrees Celsius. Do not freeze a reconstituted peptide. Most reconstituted peptides are stable for 4 to 6 weeks when stored correctly with BAC water.

Always store your vials upright. Keep them away from the fridge door where temperature fluctuations are greater.

---

### Common Mistakes to Avoid

**Using plain sterile water instead of BAC water**
Without the preservative, your reconstituted peptide can develop bacterial contamination within days. Always use BAC water.

**Shaking the vial**
This introduces air bubbles and can physically damage the peptide structure. Swirl gently instead.

**Leaving the vial at room temperature**
Peptides degrade much faster at room temperature. Once reconstituted, return the vial to the fridge immediately after drawing your dose.

**Reusing needles**
Every draw should use a fresh needle. Reusing needles introduces contamination risk and also causes the needle to blunt, making injections more uncomfortable.

**Not labelling your vials**
Once you are running more than one compound, unlabelled vials become a genuine risk. Write the compound name, date of reconstitution and concentration on each vial.

---

### A Note on Injection Technique

Subcutaneous injections are administered into the fatty tissue just beneath the skin, typically in the abdomen, thigh or outer upper arm. The technique is similar to how diabetics self-administer insulin.

Pinch a small fold of skin. Insert the needle at roughly a 45-degree angle. Inject slowly and steadily. Apply gentle pressure with the swab after removing the needle.

If you are new to self-injection, take your time. The needle gauge used for peptides (typically 29 to 31G) is very fine and most people find the injections significantly less uncomfortable than they expected.

---

### Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use pre-mixed peptide solutions?**
Some suppliers sell pre-mixed peptides in liquid form. While this is more convenient, the stability and shelf life are shorter, and you have less control over concentration. For research purposes, starting from lyophilised powder is generally preferred.

**What if I accidentally freeze my reconstituted peptide?**
It is best to avoid this. Freezing and thawing can affect peptide integrity. If this happens, inspect the solution carefully before use and be aware that potency may have been reduced.

**How do I dispose of used syringes safely?**
Used needles should be placed in a sharps container. Many pharmacies accept used sharps for safe disposal.

## Research Peptides vs Pharmaceutical Drugs: What's the Difference and Why Does It Matter?

If you have spent any time researching peptides, you have likely come across the term "research compounds" or "research chemicals." You may have also noticed that some peptides, like Semaglutide, are available as prescription medications, while others, like BPC-157, are only available through research-focused suppliers.

What is the actual difference? And why does it matter for how you source and use peptides?

This article breaks it down clearly.

---

### What Is a Pharmaceutical Drug?

A pharmaceutical drug is a compound that has passed through a formal regulatory approval process, such as the Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in the United States or the South African Health Products Regulatory Authority (SAHPRA) in South Africa.

This approval process involves:

- Preclinical testing in cell cultures and animal models
- Phase 1 clinical trials (safety testing in a small group of healthy humans)
- Phase 2 clinical trials (efficacy testing in a larger group with the target condition)
- Phase 3 clinical trials (large-scale trials comparing the drug to existing treatments or placebo)
- Regulatory review and approval

This entire process typically takes 10 to 15 years and costs hundreds of millions of rands. Once approved, a pharmaceutical drug can be legally manufactured, prescribed and sold under strict quality standards.

Examples of pharmaceutical peptides include Semaglutide (sold as Ozempic and Wegovy), insulin and various growth hormone preparations.

---

### What Is a Research Peptide?

A research peptide is a compound that has not completed the full pharmaceutical approval process. This does not mean it is untested. Many research peptides have undergone extensive preclinical research and some have been studied in human trials. But they have not received regulatory approval as medicines.

Research peptides are sold with the explicit designation that they are for research purposes only, meaning they are not approved for human use as a medicine in the regulatory sense.

This is an important legal distinction. The compound itself may have a strong body of scientific literature behind it. BPC-157, for example, has been studied for over two decades in animal models with consistently promising results. But because no pharmaceutical company has taken it through a full approval process (largely due to commercial reasons, as naturally occurring peptides cannot be easily patented), it remains a research compound.

---

### Why Are So Many Effective Peptides Still Classified as Research Compounds?

This is a question that comes up often, and the answer is mostly economic rather than scientific.

The pharmaceutical approval process is extraordinarily expensive. Companies invest in it because they can patent the resulting drug and recoup their investment through exclusive sales rights. Natural peptides are much harder to patent, which reduces the commercial incentive to run the expensive trials necessary for approval.

This means some peptides with genuinely compelling research profiles remain in a grey zone not because the science is lacking, but because the financial incentives for formal approval do not exist.

GHK-Cu, BPC-157 and TB-500 are good examples. The research on these compounds is substantial, but they remain in the research category because no commercial entity has made the investment to push them through formal approval.

---

### Key Practical Differences

| | Pharmaceutical Drug | Research Peptide |
|---|---|---|
| Regulatory approval | Yes, fully approved | No |
| Human use approved | Yes (for specific indications) | Not as a medicine |
| Available via prescription | Yes | No |
| Quality standards | Mandated by regulator | Varies by supplier |
| Clinical trial data | Extensive | Varies, often preclinical |
| Cost | Generally higher | Generally lower |
| Accessibility | Via pharmacy with prescription | Via research suppliers |

---

### Does "Research Only" Mean It Is Unsafe?

Not necessarily. The research designation is a regulatory classification, not a safety rating.

Many people use research peptides and report significant results with minimal side effects. The scientific literature on compounds like BPC-157, TB-500 and Ipamorelin is generally encouraging. But it is important to be honest about what we know and do not know:

- Most research peptides have not been studied in large-scale, long-term human trials
- Individual responses vary
- Quality control is not standardised across all suppliers

The research designation is a reason for informed caution, not panic, but it does mean you carry more personal responsibility for your decisions than you would with a prescribed pharmaceutical.

---

### Why Quality Control Matters More with Research Peptides

With pharmaceutical drugs, quality is enforced. Regulatory bodies require manufacturers to meet specific standards, and products are tested and monitored. If a pharmaceutical is substandard, the regulator can intervene.

With research peptides, quality depends entirely on the supplier. There is no regulator checking every vial. This is why third-party testing and Certificates of Analysis are so critical in this space.

A peptide from a reputable, third-party-tested supplier is a fundamentally different product from one sourced from an unverified manufacturer, even if they carry the same name on the label. Purity, concentration and sterility can all vary significantly.

At STRIATA, all our peptides are sourced from GMP-compliant manufacturers and backed by third-party Certificates of Analysis. We do not believe in selling a compound we would not use ourselves.

---

### The Case of Semaglutide: When a Peptide Becomes a Drug

Semaglutide is an interesting case because it exists on both sides of this divide.

As a pharmaceutical product (Ozempic, Wegovy), it is a fully approved GLP-1 receptor agonist prescribed for type 2 diabetes and weight management. It is produced under strict pharmaceutical standards and dispensed via prescription.

As a research compound, Semaglutide is also available from research peptide suppliers. The compound is chemically the same, but it is sold without pharmaceutical approval for use as a medicine. Quality and purity will vary by supplier, making sourcing decisions particularly important for this compound.

---

### What This Means for You

Understanding the difference between research peptides and pharmaceutical drugs helps you make more informed decisions about sourcing, dosing and risk.

If a peptide is available as a pharmaceutical product for your condition, that route involves more oversight and standardised quality. If you are working with research compounds, your due diligence on the supplier becomes critically important.

The most important questions to ask are:

1. Has this compound been tested by a third-party laboratory?
2. Is there a Certificate of Analysis available?
3. What purity level is guaranteed?
4. What do we know about the safety profile from existing research?

We will cover how to read and interpret a Certificate of Analysis in our next article.

## How to Read a Certificate of Analysis: And Why You Should Always Ask for One

If you are buying peptides and you have not seen a Certificate of Analysis, you have no idea what is actually in the vial.

That might sound blunt, but it is the reality of the research peptide market. Without independent third-party testing, any claims a supplier makes about purity and potency are just words. A Certificate of Analysis (COA) is the document that separates a trustworthy supplier from one you should avoid.

This guide explains what a COA is, what it should contain and how to read one correctly.

---

### What Is a Certificate of Analysis?

A Certificate of Analysis is a document from an accredited, independent laboratory that confirms the results of testing performed on a specific batch of a compound. It provides objective, verifiable data about the quality and composition of the peptide.

The key word is "independent." A COA is only meaningful if it comes from a third-party laboratory that has no financial relationship with the supplier. Internal testing documents produced by the manufacturer themselves are not the same thing and carry far less weight.

---

### Why Does This Matter?

The research peptide market is largely unregulated. There are no mandatory quality standards enforced by a regulatory body for compounds sold as research chemicals. This means that without testing, you genuinely do not know:

- Whether the compound is what it says it is
- What purity level it has reached
- Whether it is contaminated with solvents, heavy metals or other impurities
- Whether the concentration matches what the label claims

A high-quality COA addresses all of these concerns with data.

---

### What Should a COA Contain?

A legitimate Certificate of Analysis for a research peptide should include the following:

**1. Identity Confirmation**
The test should confirm that the compound is what it claims to be. Common methods include High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) and Mass Spectrometry (MS). These techniques compare the molecular fingerprint of the sample against a reference standard.

Look for: A confirmation statement that the compound's identity matches the reference standard.

**2. Purity Percentage**
This is the most important figure. Purity is expressed as a percentage and reflects how much of the sample is the actual target compound versus other substances.

Look for: A purity of 98% or higher. Some premium compounds may be tested at 99% or above. Anything significantly below 98% should raise questions.

**3. Testing Method**
The COA should clearly state the analytical method used. HPLC (specifically Reversed-Phase HPLC) is the gold standard for peptide purity testing. Mass Spectrometry is used to confirm molecular identity.

Look for: HPLC purity data and MS identity confirmation. If the testing method is not specified, that is a red flag.

**4. Batch or Lot Number**
The COA should reference a specific batch number that matches the batch you are purchasing. This is how you confirm the test was run on your actual product, not some other batch.

Look for: A batch/lot number on both the COA and your product label. If they do not match, ask why.

**5. Testing Date**
The date of testing matters. Older results from a previous batch are not a guarantee of the quality of a current batch.

Look for: A test date that is reasonably recent and corresponds to the batch you are purchasing.

**6. Laboratory Name and Accreditation**
The issuing laboratory should be clearly identified. Reputable labs will also hold accreditation such as ISO/IEC 17025, which is the international standard for testing laboratories.

Look for: The lab name, location and any accreditation details. You should be able to independently verify the lab exists.

**7. Heavy Metals and Solvent Residue Testing (Optional but Valuable)**
Higher-quality COAs will also include results for heavy metal contamination (lead, arsenic, mercury, cadmium) and residual solvents from the manufacturing process. This level of testing reflects a more thorough quality control approach.

---

### Red Flags to Watch For

**No COA available at all**
Any supplier that cannot or will not provide a COA should be avoided entirely. There is no legitimate reason for a quality supplier to withhold this information.

**COA provided by the manufacturer rather than a third party**
Internal testing by the same company producing the compound is not independent verification. It is not meaningless, but it is not sufficient on its own.

**Purity below 98%**
This indicates either a poorly manufactured product or an older batch that has degraded. For research purposes, sub-98% purity is not acceptable.

**No batch number or a batch number that does not match your product**
This suggests the COA may not actually correspond to the product you are buying.

**Vague or unspecified testing methods**
Legitimate COAs specify exactly how testing was conducted. "Tested for purity" without any further detail is not meaningful.

**Lab name cannot be verified**
If you search for the testing laboratory online and cannot find evidence that it exists or operates, treat that COA as unreliable.

---

### How to Verify a COA

Beyond reading the document itself, you can take additional steps to verify its legitimacy:

1. Search for the laboratory online and confirm it exists and offers analytical testing services
2. Check whether the lab holds any recognised accreditation
3. If in doubt, contact the supplier and ask direct questions about the testing process
4. Compare the batch number on the COA to the label or documentation provided with your order

Reputable suppliers welcome these questions. Suppliers who are evasive or dismissive about COA verification are telling you something important.

## The Beginner's Glossary: 25 Peptide Terms You Need to Know

The peptide world has its own language. If you are new to this space, acronyms like GH, GHS, GHRP, HPLC and COA can make even a simple product description feel like reading a foreign language.

This glossary covers 25 of the most common terms you will encounter. Bookmark it and come back whenever something does not make sense.

---

### A

**Amino Acid**
The individual molecular building blocks of proteins and peptides. There are 20 standard amino acids used by the human body. When amino acids are linked together, they form a peptide chain.

**Anabolic**
Relating to the building or synthesis of complex molecules in the body, particularly muscle tissue. Anabolic processes are driven by hormones and growth factors, including those influenced by certain peptides.

**Autocrine Signalling**
A type of cell signalling where a cell releases a chemical messenger (like a peptide) that then binds to receptors on the same cell, triggering a response within itself. Some peptides work through autocrine pathways.

---

### B

**BAC Water (Bacteriostatic Water)**
Sterile water containing 0.9% benzyl alcohol, used to reconstitute lyophilised peptides. The benzyl alcohol acts as a preservative that prevents bacterial growth in the vial after it has been opened. Always use BAC water rather than plain sterile water for reconstitution.

**Bioavailability**
The proportion of a compound that enters circulation and is able to have an active effect. Subcutaneous injection delivers high bioavailability for peptides. Oral administration typically delivers much lower bioavailability because the digestive system breaks down peptide bonds before absorption.

**BPC-157**
Body Protection Compound 157. A synthetic peptide derived from a protein found in gastric juice. Widely researched for its tissue repair, anti-inflammatory and neuroprotective properties.

---

### C

**Certificate of Analysis (COA)**
A document from an independent, accredited laboratory confirming the purity, identity and quality of a specific batch of a compound. Essential for verifying peptide quality before purchase.

**CJC-1295**
A synthetic analogue of Growth Hormone Releasing Hormone (GHRH). Used to stimulate the release of growth hormone from the pituitary gland. Often combined with Ipamorelin.

**Concentration**
The amount of peptide dissolved in a given volume of liquid after reconstitution, typically expressed in micrograms per millilitre (mcg/ml) or milligrams per millilitre (mg/ml). Accurate concentration calculation is essential for correct dosing.

---

### G

**GH (Growth Hormone)**
A hormone produced by the pituitary gland that plays a key role in growth, cell reproduction, metabolism and body composition. Many peptides are used to stimulate natural GH release rather than supplementing it directly.

**GHK-Cu**
Copper peptide GHK-Cu (glycine-histidine-lysine copper). A naturally occurring peptide with significant research supporting its role in wound healing, anti-aging and skin regeneration.

**GHS (Growth Hormone Secretagogue)**
Any compound that stimulates the secretion of growth hormone. This is an umbrella term that includes both GHRH analogues (like CJC-1295) and GHRPs (like Ipamorelin or GHRP-6).

**GHRH (Growth Hormone Releasing Hormone)**
A hormone produced in the hypothalamus that signals the pituitary gland to release growth hormone. Peptides like CJC-1295 are synthetic GHRH analogues.

**GHRP (Growth Hormone Releasing Peptide)**
A class of peptides that stimulate GH release by acting on ghrelin receptors. Examples include GHRP-6, GHRP-2 and Ipamorelin. Often used in combination with GHRH analogues for a synergistic effect.

**GLP-1 (Glucagon-Like Peptide-1)**
A hormone produced in the gut that regulates blood sugar, slows gastric emptying and reduces appetite. GLP-1 receptor agonists like Semaglutide and Tirzepatide are among the most clinically significant peptides in current use.

---

### H

**Half-Life**
The time it takes for half of a peptide to be broken down or cleared from the body. Half-life determines how frequently a compound needs to be dosed to maintain stable levels. For example, Ipamorelin has a short half-life and is typically dosed multiple times daily, while CJC-1295 with DAC has a much longer half-life.

**HPLC (High-Performance Liquid Chromatography)**
The gold standard analytical method for testing peptide purity. HPLC separates components of a mixture and quantifies them, allowing a laboratory to determine exactly what percentage of a sample is the target compound.

---

### I

**IGF-1 (Insulin-Like Growth Factor 1)**
A hormone primarily produced in the liver in response to growth hormone. IGF-1 is responsible for many of the anabolic effects attributed to GH. IGF-1 LR3 is a modified, longer-acting version used in research contexts.

**Ipamorelin**
A selective GHRP considered one of the cleanest growth hormone secretagogues available. Unlike GHRP-6, Ipamorelin does not significantly raise cortisol or prolactin levels, making it a popular choice for beginners.

---

### L

**Lyophilised**
Freeze-dried. The process by which most research peptides are preserved in powder form. Lyophilisation removes water from the compound, extending its shelf life and stability significantly compared to liquid storage.

---

### M

**Mass Spectrometry (MS)**
An analytical technique used to confirm the molecular identity of a compound. Mass spectrometry generates a "fingerprint" of the compound that can be matched to a known reference standard. Often paired with HPLC on a COA.

**Mcg / Microgram**
One millionth of a gram. Most peptide doses are measured in micrograms rather than milligrams because the active amounts are very small. Understanding this unit is essential for accurate dosing calculations.

---

### P

**Peptide**
A short chain of amino acids, typically defined as containing fewer than 50 amino acids. Peptides are smaller than proteins but larger than individual amino acids.

**Purity**
Expressed as a percentage, purity indicates what proportion of the sample is the target compound. A purity of 98% means that 98% of the material is the intended peptide, with 2% being other substances. Anything below 98% is generally considered substandard for research use.

---

### S

**Subcutaneous (SubQ)**
The layer of tissue just beneath the skin. Subcutaneous injection delivers a compound into this layer, from where it is gradually absorbed into the bloodstream. Most research peptides are administered subcutaneously using a short, fine needle.

---

### T

**TB-500 (Thymosin Beta-4)**
A naturally occurring peptide found in almost all human and animal cells. Widely researched for its role in tissue repair, reducing inflammation and promoting cell migration to injury sites.
